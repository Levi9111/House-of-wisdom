The Art of War

Sun Tzu — a strategic one-page reference — Click any chapter for detailed strategies
Strategy is the art of winning before the battle begins. Victory belongs to those who understand timing, positioning, deception, and human nature. This 2,500-year-old text is not just about warfare but about the fundamental principles of conflict, competition, and leadership applicable to business, politics, and life[citation:5].
Chapter One
Laying Plans
Victory begins with calculation, preparation, and understanding the conditions that determine success before conflict starts.
Core Principle
The art of war is governed by five constant factors that must be understood before any conflict: The Moral Law (unity between people and leaders), Heaven (seasons and weather), Earth (distances, terrain, safety), The Commander (wisdom, sincerity, courage), and Method & Discipline (organization, logistics)[citation:1][citation:5].
Strategic Application
Compare yourself with your opponent across seven key considerations to forecast victory or defeat. The side superior in most will prevail[citation:1].
  • Deception is Fundamental: "All warfare is based on deception"[citation:1][citation:2]. Appear unable when able, inactive when active, far when near, and near when far[citation:1].
  • Attack the Unprepared: "Attack him where he is unprepared, appear where you are not expected"[citation:1].
Key Insight
"The general who wins a battle makes many calculations... The general who loses a battle makes but few calculations beforehand"[citation:1]. Victory is determined in planning, not just in battle.
Chapter Two
Waging War
Prolonged conflict drains resources and morale; swift and decisive action preserves strength and advantage.
Core Principle
War is enormously expensive in resources, treasure, and morale. A protracted campaign will exhaust a state[citation:1]. Therefore, speed and efficiency are paramount.
Strategic Application
  • Avoid Prolonged Warfare: "There is no instance of a country having benefited from prolonged warfare"[citation:1][citation:2].
  • Sustain Yourself from the Enemy: "A wise general makes a point of foraging on the enemy. One cartload of the enemy's provisions is equivalent to twenty of one's own"[citation:1][citation:2]. Use captured resources to augment your strength.
  • The Ultimate Objective: "In war, then, let your great object be victory, not lengthy campaigns"[citation:1].
Chapter Three
Attack by Stratagem
The supreme form of warfare is to subdue the enemy without fighting through strategy, alliances, and psychological dominance.
Core Principle
The pinnacle of skill is to break the enemy's resistance without battle[citation:1][citation:2][citation:5]. The worst policy is to besiege walled cities, which wastes time, strength, and lives[citation:1].
The Five Essentials for Victory
He will win who:
  1. Knows when to fight and when not to fight.
  2. Knows how to handle both superior and inferior forces.
  3. Has an army animated by the same spirit throughout.
  4. Prepares himself and waits to take the enemy unprepared.
  5. Has military capacity and is not interfered with by the sovereign[citation:2][citation:5].
The Ultimate Maxim
"If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat. If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle"[citation:2][citation:5].
Chapter Nine
The Army on the March
Observe terrain, morale, and behavior to anticipate danger and maintain discipline during movement.
Core Principle
How an army camps and moves reveals its state and intentions. A skilled commander reads signs from the enemy and uses terrain to their advantage[citation:5].
Strategic Application
  • Read the Signs: "When birds rise in flight, it is an ambush. When the wild animals are startled and flee, it is a sudden attack"[citation:1].
  • Use High Ground: "Camp on high ground, facing the sun. Do not climb heights to fight. So much for mountain warfare"[citation:1].
  • Beware of Rivers & Marshes: Cross rivers quickly; do not fight in marshes; keep downstream positions[citation:1].
Chapter Ten
Terrain
Understanding ground conditions allows commanders to choose favorable positions and avoid fatal disadvantages.
Core Principle
There are six kinds of terrain: accessible, entangling, temporizing, narrow, precipitous, and distant. Each requires different tactics. Defeat can come from the ground, not just the enemy[citation:1][citation:5].
The Commander's Responsibility
"When the troops are stronger, but the general does not take advantage of it, he is guilty of incompetence"[citation:1]. A leader must know when to fight and when not to fight based on terrain and strength.
On Knowing Your Troops
"If you know the enemy and know yourself, your victory will not stand in doubt; if you know Heaven and know Earth, you may make your victory complete"[citation:1]. This expands the famous maxim to include understanding the environment.
Chapter Eleven
The Nine Situations
Different circumstances demand different responses; mastery lies in recognizing and adapting to each situation.
Core Principle
Describes nine varieties of ground (e.g., dispersive, frontier, key, focal, serious, difficult, encircled, death ground) and the specific psychological and tactical responses each requires[citation:1][citation:5].
Key Strategies
  • On 'Death Ground': "Place your army in deadly peril, and it will survive; plunge it into desperate straits, and it will come off in safety"[citation:1]. When survival is at stake, soldiers fight to the death.
  • Act with Speed and Secrecy: "Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt"[citation:2][citation:5].
Chapter Twelve
The Attack by Fire
Controlled aggression and timing can destabilize the enemy, but must be used with discipline and restraint.
Core Principle
Fire is a weapon of chaos. There are five ways to attack with fire: burn personnel, supplies, equipment, storehouses, and arsenals. It must be supported and followed up quickly[citation:1][citation:5].
Strategic Application
"In order to carry out an attack with fire, one must have means available. The material for raising fire should always be kept in readiness"[citation:1]. The conditions (dry weather, proper materials) must be right. "Do not attack simply out of anger. A sovereign cannot start a war because he is enraged"[citation:5].
Chapter Thirteen
The Use of Spies
Knowledge is the ultimate weapon; intelligence allows victory with minimal cost and maximum precision.
Core Principle
Foreknowledge—gained not by superstition but by espionage—is essential. Spies are the most vital element in war, as they enable the wise sovereign and capable general to act with certainty[citation:1][citation:5].
The Five Types of Spies
  • Local Spies: Employ enemy countrymen.
  • Inside Spies: Enemy officials you turn.
  • Double Spies: Enemy spies you convert.
  • Doomed Spies: Given false information to be captured.
  • Surviving Spies: Those who return with real intelligence.
"When all five types of spies are at work and their operations are closely integrated, this is called 'the divine manipulation of the threads' and is the ruler's most precious asset"[citation:1].
Final Wisdom
"Hence it is only the enlightened ruler and the wise general who will use the highest intelligence of the army for purposes of spying, and thereby they achieve great results"[citation:1]. Knowledge precedes every victorious action.
