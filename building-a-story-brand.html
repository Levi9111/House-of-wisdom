Building a StoryBrand

By Donald Miller

Building a StoryBrand teaches businesses how to clarify their messaging by using the principles of storytelling. Donald Miller emphasizes that customers are the hero of the story, not the brand, and companies succeed when they clearly show how they can help the hero overcome a problem. The framework is a seven-part story structure that transforms confusing marketing into clear, compelling communication that drives sales.

The StoryBrand Framework: 7 Elements

Element 1
A Character (Your Customer)
The customer is the hero of the story, not your brand. Everything revolves around their desires, challenges, and transformation.
The Core Principle
Every story needs a protagonist. In your marketing story, that protagonist is always your customer. They should be able to see themselves clearly in your messaging. Miller's research shows that when customers don't immediately understand how you can help them, they move on. Clarity begins with defining your customer avatar in specific detail.
Defining Your Character
Ask: What does my customer want? What are their internal and external desires?
  • External Desire: A practical, tangible outcome (save time, make more money, lose weight, get organized).
  • Internal Desire: An emotional or identity-based outcome (feel secure, confident, respected, at peace).
Example: A financial advisor's client wants to retire comfortably (external) and feel secure about their family's future (internal).
Practical Exercise: The One-Liner
Complete this sentence for your customer avatar: "I help [TARGET CUSTOMER] achieve [EXTERNAL DESIRE] so they can feel [INTERNAL DESIRE]." For example, "I help small business owners streamline their bookkeeping so they can feel in control and focus on growth."
Element 2
Has a Problem (The Villain)
The hero's journey is defined by conflict. A clear, specific villain (the problem) creates urgency and shows why your solution matters.
The Core Principle
Companies waste millions talking about features instead of the problem they solve. A story is boring without conflict. Your customer's problem is the villain. You must identify and name this villain clearly‚Äîit creates the dramatic tension that makes your solution valuable.
The Three Layers of Problems
Miller identifies three levels of problems that create a complete villain:
  1. The External Problem: A practical, situational issue (e.g., a leaky roof, slow software, poor sales).
  2. The Internal Problem: The frustration, stress, or insecurity caused by the external problem (e.g., feeling embarrassed by a messy home, anxious about missed deadlines).
  3. The Philosophical Problem: Why the problem is unjust or wrong on a deeper level (e.g., "hardworking people shouldn't be taken advantage of by complex systems").
Practical Exercise: Name the Villain
Give your customer's problem a simple, memorable name. Instead of "inefficient workflow," call it "The Time-Suck Trap." Instead of "financial confusion," call it "The Retirement Fog." This makes the problem tangible and your solution heroic.
Element 3
Meets a Guide (Your Brand)
Your brand is not the hero‚Äîyou are the wise guide (like Yoda, Gandalf, or Mr. Miyagi) who provides the plan, tools, and wisdom for the hero to succeed.
The Core Principle
The biggest mistake brands make is making themselves the hero. Customers are not looking for another hero; they're looking for a guide who understands their problem and has a credible plan to solve it. The guide demonstrates two qualities: Empathy ("I understand your struggle") and Authority ("I have the competence to help you win").
Demonstrating Empathy & Authority
  • Show Empathy: Use language that acknowledges the customer's frustration. "We know how stressful it is to..." or "If you're tired of..."
  • Show Authority: Don't just say you're an expert; prove it. Share testimonials, case studies, certifications, years of experience, or data-backed results.
The guide's job is to transfer confidence to the hero, making them believe victory is possible.
"When we position our customer as the hero and ourselves as the guide, we will be recognized as a trusted resource to help them overcome their challenges." ‚Äì Donald Miller
Element 4
Who Gives Them a Plan
The hero is stuck and overwhelmed. The guide provides a simple, clear plan that reduces anxiety and makes the path to success obvious.
The Core Principle
Uncertainty is the enemy of conversion. A confused mind never buys. Your customer needs a clear, step-by-step plan that answers the question, "What do I do next?" Miller outlines two types of plans:
The Two Types of Plans
  • The Process Plan: Outlines the steps the customer will take to work with you (e.g., "1. Schedule a free consult, 2. We create your custom strategy, 3. We implement together"). This reduces the fear of a complicated process.
  • The Agreement Plan: Clarifies what you commit to doing and what the customer commits to doing. This establishes clear expectations and builds trust (e.g., "We promise to deliver reports weekly; you agree to provide feedback promptly").
Practical Exercise: The 3-Step Plan
Boil down your process into three simple, logical steps. Display this clearly on your website or sales page. For example: "1. Book Your Discovery Call. 2. Get Your Custom Proposal. 3. Launch & Grow." Use clear, action-oriented language.
Element 5
That Calls Them to Action
A story needs a moment where the hero must decide. A strong, clear call to action (CTA) compels the customer to move from passive interest to active engagement.
The Core Principle
Without a call to action, your story has no climax. The customer remains a spectator. Direct calls to action create a sense of urgency and tell the customer exactly what to do to begin their transformation. Miller distinguishes between two types of CTAs.
Direct vs. Transitional CTAs
  • Direct Call to Action: Asks for the sale or major commitment ("Buy Now," "Sign Up Today," "Book a Demo").
  • Transitional Call to Action: Offers a low-risk, high-value step that builds the relationship ("Download Our Free Guide," "Watch a Short Video," "Take Our Quiz"). This is especially effective for customers who aren't ready to buy immediately.
Your website and marketing should strategically use both, guiding customers down a pathway from first contact to final sale.
Element 6
That Helps Them Avoid Failure
Stories create stakes. Clearly showing what happens if the hero does nothing (or chooses a competitor) creates powerful motivation to act.
The Core Principle
People are often more motivated to avoid pain than to gain pleasure. Clearly articulating the negative consequences of inaction (sticking with the status quo) raises the stakes of your story. It answers the customer's silent question: "What will happen if I do nothing?"
Framing Failure
Don't be vague. Describe failure in specific, relatable terms related to the three problem layers:
  • External Failure: "Your sales will continue to slump."
  • Internal Failure: "You'll keep feeling overwhelmed and frustrated."
  • Philosophical Failure: "Your team deserves better leadership."
This isn't about fear-mongering; it's about honest stakes. It makes choosing your solution the logical, safe choice.
Element 7
That Ends in Success
Paint a vivid picture of the hero's life after they've defeated the villain with your help. This vision of success is the ultimate motivation.
The Core Principle
Every great story ends with a satisfying resolution. Your marketing must show‚Äînot just tell‚Äîwhat success looks like. How is the customer's life, business, or identity transformed? Describe the positive outcomes in tangible, aspirational terms that align with their internal and external desires.
The Three Layers of Success
Mirror the three problem layers with three success layers:
  1. External Success: The practical win (e.g., "You'll save 10 hours per week," "Your revenue increases by 25%").
  2. Internal Success: The emotional/identity win (e.g., "You'll feel confident and in control," "You'll gain peace of mind").
  3. Philosophical Success: The "rightness" of the outcome (e.g., "You'll be building a legacy for your family," "You'll be leading your industry").
Use testimonials and case studies as "proof of concept" that this success is achievable.

Implementing the StoryBrand Framework

The power of StoryBrand isn't just understanding the seven elements‚Äîit's applying them systematically to your business communication.

  • 1. Create Your BrandScript: Use Miller's free online tool or a simple document to map out each of the seven elements for your primary customer avatar. This is your single source of truth for all messaging.
  • 2. Rewrite Your Website: Start with your homepage. Ensure it clearly identifies the customer (hero), names their problem (villain), positions you as the guide, presents a simple plan, has clear calls to action, states the stakes, and paints a picture of success. Most websites should be drastically simplified.
  • 3. Align All Marketing: Apply the framework to email sequences, sales pages, social media bios, and even elevator pitches. Every piece of communication should tell a cohesive part of the same story.
  • 4. Train Your Team: Ensure everyone in your company, from sales to support, understands and can articulate the StoryBrand framework. Consistent messaging builds immense brand clarity.
"Building a StoryBrand shows that the clearest messages win. When companies position the customer as the hero and themselves as the guide, they make it easy for customers to understand their value and take action."

Final Takeaway: The StoryBrand Framework is not about being clever or creative with marketing; it's about being clear. It filters out the noise and allows you to communicate with the simplicity of a great story. By implementing this system, you stop telling your customers about your amazing features and start showing them how their story has a happy ending with your help.
