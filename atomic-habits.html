Atomic Habits

An actionable one-page reference - Click any law for detailed strategies
Small habits compound into remarkable results. Focus on systems, not goals, and design your environment so the right actions become inevitable. The four laws of behavior change provide a simple set of rules for creating good habits and breaking bad ones.
Law One
Make It Obvious
Design your environment so the cues for good habits are clear, visible, and unavoidable while the cues for bad habits are hidden or removed.
Core Principle
The first step to changing behavior is awareness. We must notice our habits before we can change them. By making cues for good habits obvious and cues for bad habits invisible, we remove the need for willpower.
Implementation Strategies
  • Habit Stacking: Link new habits to existing ones (e.g., "After I pour my morning coffee, I will meditate for one minute")
  • Environment Design: Make cues of good habits visible (place workout clothes next to your bed, put fruits on the counter)
  • Implementation Intentions: Use the formula "I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION]"
  • One Space, One Use: Designate specific spaces for specific activities (e.g., only work at your desk, only sleep in bed)
Breaking Bad Habits
To break a bad habit, make the cue invisible. Remove the triggers from your environment:
  • Uninstall social media apps if you want to reduce scrolling
  • Don't keep junk food in the house
  • Turn off notifications for distracting apps
Key Insight
"You don't rise to the level of your goals, you fall to the level of your systems. The environment is the invisible hand that shapes human behavior."
Law Two
Make It Attractive
Increase motivation by associating habits with positive emotions, rewards, and identities you want to reinforce.
Core Principle
The more attractive an opportunity, the more likely it is to become habit-forming. We can make habits attractive by pairing them with something we already enjoy or by connecting them to our desired identity.
Implementation Strategies
  • Temptation Bundling: Pair an action you want to do with an action you need to do (e.g., only listen to your favorite podcast while exercising)
  • Identity Reinforcement: Focus on who you wish to become, not just what you want to achieve (e.g., "I'm a healthy person" vs "I want to lose weight")
  • Positive Peer Pressure: Join a culture where your desired behavior is normal behavior
  • Reframing Mindset: Change how you talk about habits (e.g., "I get to exercise" instead of "I have to exercise")
Breaking Bad Habits
To break a bad habit, make it unattractive. Highlight the benefits of avoiding the bad habit:
  • Create a "habit contract" with consequences for bad behavior
  • Reframe your mindset about bad habits (e.g., "Smoking makes me smell bad and costs money")
  • Find a better substitute that provides similar benefits
The Dopamine Connection
Dopamine is released not only when you experience pleasure, but when you anticipate it. This is why checking social media becomes addictive—you anticipate likes and comments. You can hack this by creating anticipation for good habits.
Law Three
Make It Easy
Reduce friction by simplifying habits, lowering resistance, and focusing on consistency rather than intensity.
Core Principle
Human behavior follows the Law of Least Effort. We naturally gravitate toward the option that requires the least amount of work. By reducing the friction associated with good behaviors, we make them easier to adopt.
Implementation Strategies
  • The Two-Minute Rule: Scale down your habits until they take two minutes or less to do (e.g., "read before bed" becomes "read one page")
  • Reduce Friction: Prime your environment for future use (e.g., set out your workout clothes the night before, keep a water bottle on your desk)
  • Master the Entry Point: The most important part of a habit is getting started; momentum builds after initiation
  • Automate Your Habits: Use technology to automate good decisions (e.g., automatic transfers to savings, meal delivery services)
Breaking Bad Habits
To break a bad habit, increase friction. Make bad habits difficult:
  • Unplug the TV after use so you have to plug it in next time
  • Delete credit card information from shopping sites
  • Keep your phone in another room while working
  • Use a smaller plate to reduce overeating
The Power of Repetition
"Habits form based on frequency, not time. Each repetition is a vote for the type of person you wish to become. Don't break the chain of consistency."
Key Insight
The most effective form of learning is practice, not planning. Focus on taking action, not being in motion. Motion makes you feel like you're getting things done without actually producing results.
Law Four
Make It Satisfying
Reinforce habits with immediate rewards so the brain associates the behavior with success and progress.
Core Principle
What is immediately rewarded is repeated. What is immediately punished is avoided. Because good habits often have delayed rewards while bad habits offer immediate gratification, we need to add immediate satisfaction to good habits.
Implementation Strategies
  • Reinforcement Rewards: Give yourself an immediate reward after completing a habit (e.g., a piece of chocolate after a workout, a few minutes of social media after focused work)
  • Habit Tracking: Use visual measures like a habit tracker or calendar chain to see your progress (the mere act of tracking can be satisfying)
  • Never Miss Twice: Give yourself permission to miss once, but never twice—this prevents the "what the hell" effect where one slip turns into a complete breakdown
  • Accountability Partners: Create immediate costs to inaction by making commitments to others
Breaking Bad Habits
To break a bad habit, make it unsatisfying. Add an immediate cost:
  • Create a habit contract with financial penalties
  • Tell friends about your commitment to create social accountability
  • Use apps that block websites and charge you money if you access them
  • Visualize the long-term consequences of the bad habit
The Cardinal Rule of Behavior Change
"What is rewarded is repeated. What is punished is avoided. You teach people what to do by what you reward. You teach yourself what to do by what you find satisfying."
Key Insight
The first three laws—make it obvious, attractive, and easy—increase the odds that a behavior will be performed this time. The fourth law—make it satisfying—increases the odds that a behavior will be repeated next time.
