Exactly What to Say

The Complete System of Magic Words by Phil M. Jones
In a world where words are the currency of influence, Exactly What to Say serves as a master key to the subconscious. Phil M. Jones reveals the precise "Magic Words"—carefully crafted psychological triggers engineered to bypass the critical mind, settle nerves, create agreement, and guide decisions without manipulation. This is not about clever talking; it's about strategic, premeditated language that works with human psychology.
FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLE
Speaking Past the Critical Mind
Understand the gatekeeper of "no" and learn how to frame your words to be received with curiosity, not defensiveness.
The Core Challenge
Everyone has an internal "Critical Mind"—a filter that instinctively seeks to protect us from risk, change, and persuasion. It's the source of automatic "no," skepticism, and objections. Traditional pitches trigger this filter immediately. Magic Words are designed to sidestep it entirely, speaking directly to the subconscious, emotional brain where decisions are truly made.
Key Strategy: Reduce Threat, Invite Curiosity
The most powerful opening phrases remove perceived pressure and commitment.
"I'm not sure if it's for you, but..."
Why it works: This phrase does three things instantly: 1) It shows empathy and lack of arrogance, 2) It gives the listener psychological permission to say "no" later, which paradoxically makes them more open to listening, and 3) It triggers curiosity ("Why wouldn't it be for me? What is it?"). It frames the conversation as an exploration, not a sales pitch.
Other Foundational Openers
To Gauge Interest Without Pressure:
"How open-minded are you about...?"
To Introduce a Big Idea:
"What would it mean to you if...?"
DECISION ACCELERATORS
Phrases That Trigger "Yes"
Specific linguistic patterns that make agreement feel like the natural, safe, and obvious next step.
The Power of Presumptive Language
Instead of asking IF someone wants to do something, frame your language around WHEN or HOW they will do it. This assumes the positive outcome and focuses the mind on implementation.
"When would be a good time to get started?"
Contrast this with the weak alternative: "Would you like to get started?" The first presumes the decision is made and moves to logistics. The second invites a reconsideration of the decision itself.
Magic Words for Common Scenarios
  • To Overcome Indecision: "If you were to say yes, what would be your biggest reason?" This forces the prospect to articulate their own positive motivation.
  • To Create a Safe "Yes": "A lot of people in your position start by..." Leverages social proof and reduces the perceived risk of the unknown.
  • The Ultimate Closing Question: "Would it be crazy if...?" (e.g., "Would it be crazy if we scheduled the first session for tomorrow?"). Makes the actual proposal seem reasonable by comparison to the "crazy" idea.
PREEMPTIVE STRATEGY
Disarming Objections
Address the concerns in someone's mind before they voice them, turning potential roadblocks into stepping stones.
The "What Would Have to Happen?" Framework
When someone raises an objection (e.g., "It's too expensive," "I need to think about it"), don't argue. Use this phrase to transform a flat "no" into a solvable problem.
"What would have to happen for this to become a no-brainer for you?"
Why it works: This bypasses debate. It accepts their concern as valid and immediately collaborates on a solution. It moves the conversation from "whether" to "how." The other person now has to think concretely about what would satisfy them, which often reveals that their objection isn't as solid as it seemed.
Pre-Framing Common Concerns
Bring up the objection yourself in a way that diminishes its power.
For Price Concerns:
"Some people look at this and their first thought is about the investment. What they later realize is that the real cost is in *not* solving this problem..."
For Procrastination:
"You might be thinking you should wait. The only thing I'd ask is, what's likely to change between now and then that would make the decision easier?"
CONVERSATION ARCHITECTURE
Framing & Controlling Choices
How you present options determines the choice that is made. Learn to structure decisions for clarity and favorable outcomes.
The Rule of Three
The human brain loves three options. One is a mandate, two is a dilemma, but three provides a comfortable middle ground. Structure your offers with three clear tiers, where your preferred option is positioned in the middle.
"Most people in your situation tend to choose Option B. It gives them the core results they're after without overcomplicating things. Option A is great if you're just testing the waters, and Option C is for when you're ready to go all-in."
The "Just Imagine" Pattern
This phrase activates the listener's visual and emotional brain, making the future benefits feel real and immediate.
"Just imagine it's six months from now. You went ahead with this, and you're experiencing [specific benefit]. How would that change things for you?"
Why it works: It forces a mental rehearsal of success. By describing a positive future state in detail, you're not just listing features; you're helping them *feel* the outcome. Once they've emotionally experienced the benefit, backing away feels like a loss.
CONVERSATION SUSTAINERS
Phrases for Momentum & Commitment
What to say after the initial "yes" to ensure action, maintain alignment, and build lasting relationships.
Securing the Next Step
Never leave a positive conversation without a clear, agreed-upon next action. Frame it as a simple, logical progression.
"The next logical step would be for us to... Does that make sense as a way forward?"
This combines presumptive language ("the next logical step") with a check for agreement ("does that make sense?"), making it collaborative rather than pushy.
The Reassurance & Investment Phrase
After someone commits, use language that reinforces their good decision and increases their psychological investment.
"Based on everything we've discussed, I'm really confident this is going to work well for you. What part are you most excited to get started on?"
Why it works: It expresses your confidence (transferring it to them), and by asking what they're "excited" about, it reframes the transaction from a cost to an anticipated gain. It makes them re-articulate their positive reasons, solidifying their commitment.
"The right words at the right time can open doors and influence decisions. Success in conversation isn't about being clever in the moment; it's about being prepared for the moments that matter. Have your Magic Words ready before you need them."
